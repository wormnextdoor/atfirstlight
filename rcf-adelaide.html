INTERVIEW OF ADELAIDE AHMED 

BY DET. LEBLANC: 

Q. Adelaide. It’s a pleasure to meet you. I’m sorry for your loss.

A. Save it. Your sympathy won’t bring my sister back.

Q. We’re doing everything we can to bring her to justice.

A. Really? Because here you are, wasting time with small talk. Get to it.

Q. Were you and River close?

A. Of course we were. She was my little sister. Even when I left for university, we used to call every weekend unless one of us was busy. I helped her with her homework from afar, and we’d play video games on call. We were as close as siblings can be.

Q. Did she mention any plans for the day she disappeared?

A. No. She got super busy in the few weeks before her disappearance, so we didn’t talk much. Last I heard from her, she was struggling to cope with her friend’s death.

Q. Florian Elster’s suicide, correct?

A. Yeah. She was so sure that they’d have told her if they were suicidal. I didn’t try to reason with her because I knew she wouldn’t listen. She was hard-headed like that; I loved her and hated her for it at the same time. So, I told her to do whatever she thought was right.

Q. Do you know what she did?

A. She started to investigate. She didn’t tell me that, but I know that’s what she did. She was so sure that they were murdered, and I figured that if she started investigating, she’d realize that’s not what happened, and it might give her closure. I don’t know. I get why she felt the way she did.

Q. Tell me more about that.

A. ...Someone important to our family was murdered a long time ago, but the case was never closed, and we’re all still hung up on it. She must’ve been scared that history would repeat itself with Florian’s death. Plus, because of her ADHD, she had a lot of trouble staying organized. Our parents thought she was immature and irresponsible for that. I never saw her that way, but I know a lot of other people did, too. I think she saw the investigation as a chance to prove herself to them, though she might not have realized that.

Q. I see. So, she had trouble with your parents?

A. I wouldn’t put it that way. They just underestimated her a lot. But otherwise, everything was fine. River had a great relationship with them, especially with our mom.

Q. Do you know of anyone who might’ve wanted to hurt her?

A. If I did, I’d have mentioned it by now. No, I can’t think of anyone.

Q. She was openly gay, wasn’t she?

A. Yeah, she was a lesbian.

Q. Did anyone ever give her trouble for that?

A. Not really. I mean, kids at school said some stuff to her about it, but it never went far, and she didn’t let it bother her. I highly doubt that her death was a hate crime.

Q. Do you know anything about a fight she got into shortly before her death?

A. A fight? What happened?

Q. Your mother mentioned that she saw someone being attacked on the way home one day, and she intervened.

A. Oh. No, I don’t know anything about that. It must’ve happened after I last called her.

Q. When was that?

A. Uh... I’m not sure. It should be saved in my call history, though.

Q. Mind if I take a look?

A. Sure, whatever.

Q. ...Perfect. Thank you, Adelaide. That’s all.

A. You better find whoever did this. Make them pay.

Q. I’ll do my best.

A. I don't want your best, I was results. Make. Them. Pay.

Q. That’s enough, Adelaide. Good night.
